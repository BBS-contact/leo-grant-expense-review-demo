# What Is LEO?

LEO is a human-controlled institutional review and evidence accountability system.

It is designed to help organizations detect weak records, incomplete documentation, unusual patterns, missing evidence, and review-worthy institutional risks before they become audit, governance, donor, legal, or financial problems.

LEO does not replace human judgment.

LEO does not issue final decisions.

LEO supports responsible people by turning institutional uncertainty into structured evidence, reviewer questions, traceable findings, and accountable review workflows.

---

# Why LEO Exists

Many institutions do not fail because one person immediately sees a problem and ignores it.

They fail because important signals are fragmented across:

* invoices,
* contracts,
* expense records,
* supplier data,
* budget lines,
* reporting rules,
* internal notes,
* audit requirements,
* and human decisions.

By the time a problem becomes visible, it may already be too late.

LEO is being developed to help institutions see review-worthy signals earlier, preserve evidence, ask the right questions, and keep human responsibility clear.

---

# What Problem LEO Solves

LEO addresses a practical institutional problem:

```text
records exist,
but accountability is often unclear.
```

A document may exist, but no one knows whether it is sufficient.

A supplier may appear in records, but no one has checked whether the relationship, amount, activity, and documentation make sense together.

An expense may be booked, but its connection to a budget line, grant activity, or reporting rule may be weak.

An audit may happen months later, when the original context is already lost.

LEO helps preserve that context while the review is still possible.

---

# What LEO Does

LEO currently focuses on evidence-backed institutional review workflows.

It can:

* load structured institutional records,
* check input quality,
* generate review signals,
* identify unusual or incomplete records,
* connect findings with rule references,
* preserve evidence lineage,
* display reviewer questions,
* support human review actions,
* track attachment-level document review states,
* create institutional readiness blockers,
* and export human review packages.

In the current Grant Expense Review prototype, LEO can show:

* how many findings require review,
* which findings are high-priority,
* which expenses need more evidence,
* which attachments require replacement,
* which items should be escalated,
* and whether an institutional handoff is ready or blocked.

---

# What LEO Does NOT Do

LEO is intentionally not an autonomous decision-maker.

LEO does NOT:

* issue fraud verdicts,
* issue legal conclusions,
* issue donor compliance decisions,
* approve or reject payments,
* punish suppliers,
* mutate production records,
* mutate canonical registries,
* sign documents,
* access keys,
* perform external execution,
* replace accountants,
* replace auditors,
* replace lawyers,
* or replace institutional management.

LEO creates review support.

Humans remain responsible for interpretation, decisions, approvals, and institutional actions.

---

# Core Principle

LEO converts institutional uncertainty into a structured review chain:

```text
unclear record
→ evidence-backed signal
→ reviewer question
→ human review action
→ readiness guidance
→ exportable review package
```

This makes institutional review more transparent, less chaotic, and easier to audit.

---

# Current Prototype State

LEO currently has local working prototypes for:

* procurement and accounting anomaly review,
* grant expense review,
* evidence-backed findings,
* rule traceability,
* attachment review,
* institutional handoff readiness,
* and human review package export.

The current prototypes are local-first and non-production.

They demonstrate the workflow without connecting to production systems, executing institutional actions, or performing autonomous enforcement.

---

# Why This Matters

Organizations often need help before a formal scandal, audit failure, donor dispute, or governance crisis appears.

LEO is designed for that earlier moment.

It helps institutions ask:

* What looks unusual?
* What evidence exists?
* What evidence is missing?
* Who should review this?
* What decision has a human actually made?
* Is this ready for institutional handoff?
* What should be preserved for audit memory?

This is not about replacing people.

It is about giving responsible people a structured way to see, review, explain, and preserve institutional decisions.

# Practical Positioning

LEO should be understood as:

```text
an institutional integrity and evidence review assistant
```

not as:

```text
an autonomous AI authority
```

The system is being developed to support foundations, civic organizations, public-interest projects, grant-funded institutions, and eventually broader governance environments where evidence, accountability, and review traceability matter.
